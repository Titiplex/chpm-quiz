## Short notice before starting

You are invited to complete **[questionnaire/study/service name]**. The purpose is **[specific purpose]**. The organization responsible for this processing is **[data controller and contact]**. The expected completion time is approximately **[duration]**.

Your link contains a unique secret token and expires on **[expiry/date rule]**. Do not forward or share it. You may pause and resume before final submission. After you confirm final submission, your answers are locked and cannot be changed through this link.

Your answers are associated with a public pseudonymous code. Your **[email/phone, when collected]** is stored separately in an encrypted identity area and is not shown in ordinary questionnaire, moderation, or statistics screens. Because an authorized correspondence can still exist, the data is pseudonymized, not anonymous.

The system may record limited technical events such as page navigation, answer changes, help-panel openings, and response duration for **[approved purpose]**. Team notifications do not include the content of your answers.

Please avoid entering names, email addresses, phone numbers, exact addresses, exact dates, or other directly identifying details in free-text answers unless the question and notice explicitly require them.

## Mandatory UX requirements

- Show purpose, controller, duration, pseudonymization status, token confidentiality, final-submission effect, telemetry, retention, rights, DPO/privacy contact, and support route before response collection.
- Use language appropriate to the audience and provide accessible/localized alternatives.
- Do not use a preselected consent box or bundle optional processing with required processing.
- Record the exact notice/version shown when the legal basis requires evidence.
- Keep a persistent route back to privacy/contact information during the questionnaire.
- Warn before free text and before irreversible submission.
- Distinguish research participation/clinical care/administrative requirement accurately; never overstate voluntariness or anonymity.
